The Silent Era

The first feature film made in India was Pundalik by N.G. Chitre and R.G Torney which screened on the 18th of May, 1912. In the year of 1913, Dada Saheb Phalke made the first silent feature film Raja Harrishchandra. This film is based on a popular story about an upright righteous ancient king and the sacrifices he made to remain firm in his convictions. While Pundalik is India’s first feature-length film, Raja Harrishchandra is the first full-length commercial Indian feature film.

The response to this film was huge because for a movie viewer, it was a surreal moment to see an imagined past become real. The success of this film prompted Phalke to form a company by the name of Hindustan Films and made two other feature films Mhoni Bhamasur and Satyavan Savitri. Phalke proved that making films and screening them for a paying audience was a profitable business venture and attracted new investors.

Films made in Hollywood were a main point of reference but themes were adapted to suit the Indian viewer. In 1936, Himanshu Rai set up the famous Bombay Talkies, which was a studio for establishing the ever-popular genres of musical romance and melodrama. Indian cinema has six influences when making films. These six are: stories from Indian mythology, folk theatres, the Parsee theatre, Hollywood films, and the Western Musical. Around this time, India was still ruled by the British, but the British government introduced the Indian Cinematograph Act. This act provided censorship and licensing that would control the contents of a film. The silent era of Indian film history was not necessarily “silent.” Live music bands accompanies film in order to make the experience more unique different for viewers.
